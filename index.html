Important Changes to the U.S. Constitution

Amendment V

The Fifth Amendment

Summary

You can't be forced to testify against yourself in court. The government can't try you twice for the same crime or take your property without paying you fairly.
This Amendment is where the idea of "Innocent Until Proven Guilty" was established.

Why It Was Added

Added in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights. The Founders wanted to protect people from being forced to confess like they were in England.

Key Case

Miranda v. Arizona (1966) - This is why police read you your rights when you're arrested. The Supreme Court said police have to tell people they can stay silent.

Today

People use this right all the time in court to avoid answering questions that might make them look guilty. It protects everyone's rights during criminal investigations.

Amendment XIII

The Thirteenth Amendment

Summary

Slavery and forced labor are illegal in the United States, except as punishment for crimes.

Why It Was Added

Ratified in 1865 after the Civil War. Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation freed some slaves, but this amendment ended slavery everywhere in the country permanently.

Controversy

The "Punishment" Exception - The amendment allows slavery as punishment for crime. After the Civil War, many Southern states used this to arrest Black people and force them to work.

Today

This amendment is used to fight human trafficking. Some people debate whether prison labor systems are fair because of the "punishment" exception.

Amendment XVIII

The Eighteenth Amendment

Summary

Banned making, selling, and transporting alcohol. This was called Prohibition. It was repealed by the 21st Amendment in 1933.

Why It Was Added

Ratified in 1919. People believed alcohol caused poverty, crime, and family problems. They thought banning it would make society better.

What Happened

Organized Crime - People didn't stop drinking. Gangsters like Al Capone made millions selling illegal alcohol. Secret bars called speakeasies opened everywhere.

Today

Prohibition shows that banning something doesn't always work and can create bigger problems. It's the only amendment that was completely repealed. People use it as an example in debates about drug laws.
